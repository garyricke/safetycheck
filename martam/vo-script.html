CH 00Welcome to Martam~40 sec
Title card: “Welcome to the Family.”

Welcome to Martam.

For more than fifty years, this has been a family company — started by Tam and Maria Kutrovatz back in 1973, and still run by their family today.

When you put on that hi-vis vest, you become part of that family. And in this family, one rule comes before all the others: everybody goes home safe. Every shift. No exceptions.

That's what the next few minutes are really about. Not paperwork — the real habits that keep you, and the person working next to you, out of harm's way.

Let's get into it.

CH 01General Safety Requirements~60 sec
Title card: “Every Job, Every Time.”

Before you pick up a single tool, take a beat and study the job.

Look at the task, the tools, the equipment, and the ground around you. Where are the hazards? What's your plan if something goes sideways? Ten seconds of thinking beats ten weeks of healing.

Obey every warning sign, label, and barricade. They're not decoration — they're there because somebody, somewhere, learned the hard way.

And this is the big one: if you see something unsafe — damaged gear, a bad setup, a near miss — report it to your supervisor right away. Don't walk past it. Don't assume the next guy will catch it.

See it. Say it. That's how we fix small problems before they turn into injuries.

CH 02Personal Protective Equipment~90 sec
Title card: “Your Last Line of Defense.”

Your PPE is the last thing standing between you and a bad day — so let's get it right, head to toe.

Start at the top. A hard hat, worn at all times, on every project. Non-conductive, with your name and the Martam logo on it. No exceptions.

Your eyes. Safety glasses any time there's dust, debris, or flying fragments. Goggles when you're dealing with chemical splash or torch cutting. And a face shield — over your glasses — for grinding, chipping, or cut-off saws.

Your ears. In high-noise areas, plugs or muffs, every time. Hearing doesn't grow back.

Your lungs. Dust masks or respirators whenever there are fumes, dust, or vapors in the air.

Your hands. Work gloves for the rough stuff, and the right specialty gloves for chemicals or heat.

And your feet. Sturdy leather work boots — steel-toe when you're running jackhammers, tampers, or heavy equipment.

One more thing, on clothing: shirts with at least a four-inch sleeve, and long pants. No shorts, no tank tops. Dress for the job — not the weather.

CH 03Fall Protection~75 sec
Title card: “Six Feet Is Too High to Fall.”

Falls are one of the fastest ways to get seriously hurt on a site — so here's the line in the sand.

Any time you're working six feet or more above the ground, fall protection is mandatory. Not “if you feel like it.” Mandatory.

First choice is guardrails — a solid barrier on open sides, edges, and elevated surfaces, between you and the drop.

When guardrails aren't possible, you wear a full-body harness and lanyard, tied off to an approved anchor point. Moving along an elevated run? Use a static line so you stay tied off the whole way across.

And one hard rule: no safety belts. Ever. They cause more injury than they prevent — a full-body harness only.

Every site with heights has a fall-protection plan. Know it before you go up. Because six feet up, with nothing to catch you, is six feet too high.

CH 04Electrical & Power Tools~75 sec
Title card: “Inspect Before You Trust It.”

Tools make the work possible — and they'll hurt you fast if you get careless.

Before every use, inspect it. Check the cord, the plug, the guards, the casing. See any damage or wear? Take it out of service and tag it for repair. Don't “just finish this one cut.” A defective tool can mean a shock, a fire, or worse.

Every tool needs to be properly grounded or double-insulated. Keep the guards in place — they're not optional, and they are never something you modify or remove.

Electrical repairs are for qualified electricians only. An unauthorized fix puts everyone downstream of you at risk.

And powder-actuated tools? Those take specific training before you ever pull the trigger.

Match your PPE to the tool — gloves, eyes, ears — and let the guard do its job.

CH 05Fire Prevention~55 sec
Title card: “Know the Plan Before You Need It.”

Fire moves fast, so know your plan before you ever need it.

Three things to lock in on day one. Where the emergency phone numbers are posted. Where the fire extinguishers are. And — on projects that require it — your evacuation routes, alarm signals, and meeting area.

Extinguishers get inspected regularly, so they're charged and ready when it counts. Only use one if you're trained and it's a small, controllable fire.

If it's bigger than that — get out, get everyone out, and report it. No fire is worth your life.

CH 06Ladders & Material Handling~80 sec
Title card: “Climb Smart, Lift Smarter.”

Two everyday tasks hurt more people than you'd think: climbing and lifting.

Ladders first. Extension ladders go up at about a four-to-one angle, and extend at least three feet above the landing — set on firm, level ground, and secured when you can. Step ladders: open them all the way, lock the spreaders, and stay off the top two steps.

Now lifting. Bend your knees, keep your back straight, and hold the load close to your body — no twisting. If it's heavy or awkward, get help, or grab a forklift or hoist. There's no trophy for wrecking your back.

And when you stack materials, mind the load limits and keep it stable, so nothing shifts or topples on somebody.

Work smart, not sore.

CH 07Flagging & Traffic~55 sec
Title card: “Never Turn Your Back on Traffic.”

When we work in and around live traffic, flagging is what keeps everyone alive — you, the crew, and the driving public.

If you're flagging, you're trained for it first. Every time.

Wear your high-vis so drivers can see you clearly — especially in low light or bad weather. Position yourself where you're visible, and where you've got an escape path.

And the rule you never break: never turn your back on traffic. Always face the oncoming cars, so you can see trouble coming and move.

A distracted driver won't be looking out for you. So you look out for you.

CH 08Housekeeping~50 sec
Title card: “A Clean Site Is a Safe Site.”

A clean site is a safe site. It's that simple.

Clutter causes trips, slips, and fires, so keep your area orderly as you work. Trash goes in the approved containers — not on the ground.

See a nail sticking out of a board? Pull it, or bend it over, before it finds someone's hand or foot. Handle your scrap carefully.

Good housekeeping isn't busywork. It's how we cut out the injuries nobody sees coming.

CH 09First Aid & Injury Reporting~90 sec
Title card: “Report It — Every Time.”

If someone gets hurt, here's exactly what you do.

First — make sure the area is safe before you step in. You can't help anyone if you become the second victim.

Get help fast. Call it out on the radio, and know where the emergency numbers are posted. Only give first aid if you're trained and it's safe to do so — don't make an injury worse. Know where the first aid kits are, and who your trained responders are.

Now the part that protects you: report every injury immediately — no matter how small. A scratch today can be an infection next week. And if you realize later that you got hurt on the job, report it the moment you know.

Any accident — injury or property damage — gets reported right away. Sign the Accident and Injury log at the end of every shift. And if there's an investigation, take part, and be straight about what happened.

Reporting isn't about blame. It's how we stop the same thing from hurting the next person.

Title card: “Welcome to the Team.”

Last thing.

Before you start work, you'll review and sign the Safety Orientation Acknowledgment form. Your signature says three things: you understand the rules we just covered, you'll follow them on every job, and you'll report unsafe conditions, incidents, and hazards when you see them.

That's the deal — and it goes both ways. You look out for the crew, and this crew looks out for you.

Welcome to the team. We're glad you're here.

Now let's get to work — and let's all get home safe.
